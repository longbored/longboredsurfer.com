I would've been satisfied with something maybe a quarter this length. As it stands, this was way too long for my tastes. That opinion might be influenced by the number of WWII-related books I've consumed lately. William L. Shirer, again, feels inexhaustible in his writing. I do value his first-hand experiences and interviews - he's truly one of a kind in that regard.

Still, if I were to summarize the book, it boils down to this: the French...

  • Rested on the laurels of WWI victory, ignoring advice to fight differently. Their tanks were, on paper, superior to Germany's - but they failed to use them effectively, clinging to static defenses like the Maginot Line and utterly mishandling the Ardennes.
  • Let political self-interest override national planning. Short-term reelection goals drove bad decisions.
  • Grossly misjudged Germany's leader and fell for a string of lies - like so many other governments did.
  • Failed to grasp the danger of fascism. Focused inward, they neglected foreign policy and missed the opportunity to build stronger alliances.

You're welcome - I just saved you 1,000+ pages (or 48 hours of listening).
