This book was very well written. But reading this book now completely satisfies me knowing anything more about Custer. My understanding of him in "Crazy Horse and Custer" carries through here - deeply insecure, yet full of bravery. His gambling and womanizing plays more of a part in this book. The author payed less attention to Custer's last-in-his-class distinction, and focused more on him being highly educated. Custer's continued insecurities and unsavory habits contribute to me not wanting to get to know him any more. And despite him being anti-slavery, he had some extremely racist tendencies, and actively supported further unsavory actions. Great writing. Not a great guy.

At this point, it's been two weeks since I finished this book, but this quote has been on my mind ever since:

"A man who lies to himself is often the first to take offense," Dostoevsky wrote in The Brothers Karamazov. Lying to oneself is a nearly universal human trait, to one degree or another. But some consciousness of the truth usually lurks; reminders make the liar brittle and defensive;

While Custer's tendency toward self-deception was non-zero, this quote is more for reference with others than with Custer himself.
