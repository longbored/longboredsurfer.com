My inadequate attempt to answer the title's question, based on what I learned or was reminded of by this book: "It's at least partially because race, especially in America, can be complicated, and presents black kids with experiences and thoughts that are sometimes easier to share between fellow black kids. It's not personal. Probably. And no - they're not colluding. Get over yourself." I've read a variety of other books on this topic (or adjacent topics), and this is an easy one to digest. I appreciate the perspective shared by Dr. Tatum, and the deep research and statistics presented. That said, it's the abundance of those statistics in the latter half or third of the book that got to be overwhelming to the point of them blending together. It doesn't make the issues presented any less valid or concerning.
